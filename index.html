Hidden Gems of Wayanad

Hidden Places

Pakshipathalam — The Birds' Rock Cave

Buried deep inside the Brahmagiri forest range, Pakshipathalam is a network of caves and rock formations accessible only by a strenuous 7 km trek. The name literally means "dwelling place of birds," and the place earns it — the forest canopy hosts rare hornbills, Malabar grey hornbills, and countless species barely seen elsewhere.

"Not a tourist spot — it's a proper wilderness expedition. You need permits, a forest guide, and a genuine willingness to get muddy. Most visitors give up halfway, which means the people who make it have the caves entirely to themselves."
📍 Brahmagiri Hills, ~30km from Mananthavady 🎫 Forest permit required 🥾 Strenuous trek

Phantom Rock (Cheengeri Mala) — The Skull on the Hill

A natural rock formation near Sulthan Bathery that bears an uncanny resemblance to a human skull — depending on the angle you look at it. The short trek through scrub forest to reach it is half the fun, with panoramic views of paddy fields and misty Western Ghats ridges stretching into Karnataka.

"Locals call it Cheengeri Mala. The 'phantom' name is a recent tourist invention. But the rock genuinely looks like a skull. Best seen at sunrise when the morning mist is still low and dramatic."
📍 Near Sulthan Bathery 🌅 Best at sunrise 🆓 Free entry

Thollayiram Kandi — The Secret Valley

A hidden valley hemmed in by misty mountains, almost entirely unknown to outsiders. The hiking trails are surprisingly gentle, and the summit offers a panorama of overlapping ridgelines disappearing into clouds. No tea stalls, no selfie spots — just silence and the occasional distant sound of a waterfall.

"This is what Wayanad looked like before Instagram. Locals here are genuinely surprised to see visitors. Go before it's 'discovered.'"
📍 Interior Wayanad 🥾 Easy-moderate trek 🧭 Local guide recommended

Kanthanpara Falls — Wayanad's Quiet Waterfall

While Soochipara and Meenmutty attract the crowds, Kanthanpara sits near Meppadi without a lot of fanfare. Surrounded by thick forest and rocky terrain, the waterfall flows into a calm pool that's perfect for wading. The absence of crowds makes it feel genuinely wild.

"October to January is the sweet spot — good water flow, no monsoon access issues, and you might have the pool to yourself on a weekday."
📍 Near Meppadi 🗓️ Oct–Jan best 💧 Swimming possible

Karlad Lake — The Forgotten Lake

Wayanad's second-largest freshwater lake sits about 15 km from Kalpetta, largely ignored in favour of the more photogenic Pookode Lake. Managed by DTPC, it offers kayaking, bamboo rafting, zip-lining, and rock climbing — a full adventure day with almost no tourist buzz.

"Pookode gets all the brochure space. Karlad gets all the action. The bamboo rafting here is genuinely good fun and the lake views are stunning."
📍 ~15km from Kalpetta 🚣 Kayaking & rafting 🏕️ Family friendly

Tholpetty Wildlife Sanctuary — The Unbranded Safari

Muthanga Wildlife Sanctuary gets all the tourist packages, but Tholpetty — a quieter section of the Wayanad Wildlife Sanctuary near the Kerala-Karnataka border — offers equally rich sightings: elephants, sambar, leopards, wild dogs — with far smaller jeep queues. It's less commercialized but the animals don't know the difference.

"The dawn safaris here are something else. No noise, no crowds. You hear the forest waking up, which is the whole point."
📍 Near Thirunelli 🐘 Elephant sightings 🌄 Dawn safaris

Strange History

The Chain Tree of Lakkidi — Ghost of a Betrayed Guide

Near the Thamarassery Ghat pass at Lakkidi, a large ancient fig tree stands wrapped in heavy chains. The story behind it is one of Wayanad's most haunting: a tribal chieftain named Karinthandan (who lived around 1700–1750) helped a British engineer discover a safe route through the treacherous ghats — and was murdered for it to prevent him from sharing the credit.

"After his death, accidents plagued travellers on that stretch. A priest was summoned, rituals performed, and his spirit was symbolically 'chained' to the tree to stop the hauntings. The tree is still there. The chain is still real. Local drivers still offer a quick prayer before the ghat descent."
📍 Lakkidi, NH-212 🕰️ c. 1700–1750 AD 🌟 Folklore landmark

Strange History

Sulthan Bathery's Jain Temple — A Name from an Ammo Dump

The town of Sulthan Bathery gets its name from a rather peculiar piece of history. A 13th-century Jain temple built in the Vijayanagara style — granite pillars, carved mandapas, serene Tirthankaras — was converted by Tipu Sultan into an ammunition battery (a "bathery") during his march to the Malabar coast. The town took the Sultan's battery as its name.

"Wayanad was once a Jain stronghold along the spice trade routes. Almost no one talks about this. The temple itself is a protected monument, still standing quietly in a leafy compound — one of the very few Jain temples in all of Kerala."
📍 Sulthan Bathery town 🏛️ 13th century 🆓 ASI protected

Edakkal Caves — Stone Age Graffiti

Edakkal's engravings are among India's oldest — dating back over 5,000 years to the Neolithic era, with some layers from the Bronze Age. The carvings depict human figures, animals, tools, and symbols that hint at trade route markers and ritual art. What's striking: the same cave was used across thousands of years by different peoples, creating a palimpsest of civilizations.

"The caves were 'discovered' by the British in 1894, but local tribes had known about them forever. Many of the symbols remain undeciphered. Some believe the writings are proto-Dravidian script — if true, it would rewrite South Indian history."
📍 Near Sulthan Bathery 🗓️ 5000+ years old 🎫 Entry fee applies

Strange History

Pazhassi Raja's Guerilla War — The Lion of Kerala

Between 1795 and 1805, Kerala Varma Pazhassi Raja waged a decade-long guerilla resistance against the British East India Company using Wayanad's forests as his battlefield. His army included Kurichiya tribal archers — arguably among the most formidable forest fighters the British encountered in South India. He was never captured. He died in battle in 1805.

"His tomb near Mananthavady is a quiet, mostly overlooked monument. No elaborate memorial, no major tourist signage. Just a modest shrine where one of Kerala's greatest freedom fighters rests — fighting the British 150 years before independence."
📍 Tomb near Mananthavady 🗓️ 1795–1805 🏹 Kurichiya warriors

Ambalavayal Heritage Museum — Where Hero Stones Tell War Stories

This under-visited museum in Ambalavayal, 12 km from Sulthan Bathery, holds one of Kerala's largest collections of tribal artefacts — stone weapons, 14th–16th century sculptures, Megalithic pottery, terracotta figures, and haunting "Veerakallu" (hero stones). These carved memorial rocks were erected on the tombs of warriors — including some that commemorate people who caught cattle thieves.

"The museum has four galleries: Veerasmruthi (warriors), Devasmruthi (deities), Gothrasmruthi (tribes), and Jeevanasmruthi (life). Entry is ₹20. Most tourist groups walk past it to get to Edakkal Caves next door."
📍 Ambalavayal, ~12km from Sulthan Bathery 🎫 Entry: ₹20 🏛️ Open all days

Food Joints

1980's A Nostalgic Restaurant — An Old Tharavadu Reborn

Housed in a converted old tharavadu (ancestral home) near Vythiri, this restaurant is all wooden beams, vintage photographs, and the smell of coconut oil and spices. The menu leans deep into Malabari — fish curry with rice, Malabar paratha, buffalo leg curry, and goat brain for the adventurous. Locals and long-haul travellers both swear by it.

"It's the kind of place where the menu is more of a suggestion. Ask what's fresh that day. The sadhya on weekends is extraordinary."
📍 Vythiri, Sentinel Rock Estates Rd 🍽️ Malabari cuisine 💰 Budget-friendly

The Coffee Grove — Appam & Stew Among Plantations

Located at Chuzhali near Bavali, this restaurant is set on a working estate that grows the very coffee it serves. The speciality is appam with vegetable stew — a classic Kerala breakfast that tastes completely different when the coconut milk is fresh and the appam is made to order in the cool hill air.

"There's something about eating appam with stew while looking out at a coffee plantation in the morning mist. No amount of restaurant ambiance can replicate that."
📍 Chuzhali, Bavali ☕ Estate coffee 🌄 Morning visits ideal

Jubilee Restaurant, Sultan Bathery — Malabar Biryani Done Right

A no-frills, well-established culinary landmark in Sultan Bathery that serves authentic Malabar cuisine: chicken dum biryani, beef biryani, sea food chatti choru, prawn masala. The "chatti choru" (earthen pot rice) with seafood is the dish locals point to when asked what to order. Often crowded on weekends but the quality holds.

"The biryani here is Malabar-style — fragrant, not spice-bomb heavy, with tender meat. It's the kind of biryani that's been made the same way for 30 years and doesn't need to change."
📍 Sultan Bathery town 🍖 Non-veg specialist ⭐ Local favourite

The Bungalow Restaurant — Heritage Bungalow Dining

Set inside an old colonial-era bungalow in Vythiri, this restaurant leans into old-world charm — vintage decor, stone floors, and a menu that blends local Kerala dishes with Continental influences. The pepper chicken curry is consistently excellent. The porrotta (layered flatbread) is properly flaky. Worth the stop if you're passing through Vythiri.

"The building itself is the story. There's something about eating in a century-old bungalow that used to house British estate managers. The aesthetic is effortless because it's genuine."
📍 Old Vythiri 🏡 Heritage property 🍽️ Kerala + Continental

Olan Restaurant — Nadan Kerala Classics

A newer spot that's become a word-of-mouth favourite among Wayanad's food community. Dedicated to traditional Kerala nadan (country-style) cooking — dishes like olan (ash gourd and coconut milk), erissery, kaalan, and paal parotta that are disappearing from most mainstream menus. The chefs here are deeply protective of these recipes.

"Paal parotta is one of those dishes that requires patience to make — soaking fried parotta in sweetened milk. Most places won't bother. This one does."
📍 Wayanad 🌱 Nadan cuisine 🤫 Word-of-mouth spot

Tribal Culture

Kurichiya Tribe — Archers Who Fought the British

The Kurichiya tribe, locally called "Malai Brahmins" or Hill Brahmins, sit at the top of Wayanad's tribal hierarchy. Their name comes from "kuri vechavan" — "he who took aim." They constituted Pazhassi Raja's guerilla army and were feared by the British for their archery. The descendants of those warriors are still expert archers, and their martial traditions are still practiced in villages today.

"They are also known for 'Nellukuthu pattu' — a harvest song-dance performed during paddy threshing. Small landowners, deeply proud people. If you want to experience genuine Wayanad, spending time in a Kurichiya village is where to start."
🏹 Expert archers 🎵 Nellukuthu pattu 📍 Mananthavady region

Tribal Culture

Paniya Tribe — Dance, Devotion, and a Slave History

The Paniyas are one of Wayanad's largest tribal communities. "Paniya" means "worker" — they were historically the labouring community of the region, often treated as bonded labour until the mid-20th century. They are celebrated for their elaborate, hypnotic dance forms that enact stories from their oral tradition. Witnessing a Paniya performance in a village setting is a genuinely moving experience.

"Unlike tourist-packaged cultural shows, Paniya dances in their own settlements are communal events — extended, participatory, and completely different from anything you'd see in a hotel ballroom."
🎵 Oral dance traditions 🌾 Agricultural heritage 📍 Throughout Wayanad

Uravu Bamboo Craft Village — Where the Forest Becomes Art

Uravu is a grassroots social enterprise near Thirunelli that employs tribal communities in bamboo craft and sustainable design. Their workshop produces furniture, household items, and architectural elements using local bamboo — and allows visitors to watch craftspeople at work. The Kurumba tribe, known for their basketry and pottery, are among the artisans working here.

"Uravu started as a livelihood project for displaced tribal families. It became something much larger — an internationally recognized model for bamboo craft. Their products are genuinely beautiful."
📍 Near Thirunelli 🎋 Bamboo crafts 🛒 Products for sale

Varambetta Mosque — 700 Years of Faith in the Hills

One of Wayanad's oldest mosques, the Varambetta Mosque near Kalpetta dates back around 700 years and is an example of the Indo-Saracenic architectural style. The building itself is less dramatic than its age suggests — which is precisely the point. It sits in quiet surroundings, largely unknown to tourists, a remnant of Wayanad's forgotten history as a crossroads of spice traders and Islamic merchants from the coast.

"The spice trade brought Arab and Mappila Muslim merchants deep into the Western Ghats centuries before European colonization. This mosque is that history made physical."
📍 Near Kalpetta 🏛️ ~700 years old 🕊️ Still active

Thirunelli Temple — The Kashi of the South

Deep in the forests of Brahmagiri Hills, the Thirunelli Temple dedicated to Lord Vishnu is over a thousand years old. It's one of Kerala's most important pilgrimage sites but remains relatively unknown outside South India. Locals call it "Kashi of Kerala" — the Papanasini river flowing next to it is believed to wash away the sins of the deceased when their ashes are scattered here.

"The forest location is the thing. The temple sits inside dense wilderness and the approach road on a monsoon morning — fog, birdsong, wet jungle — is an experience entirely separate from the religious significance."
📍 Brahmagiri Hills 🛕 1000+ years old 🌿 Forest location

Before you go

Traveller Tips

🗓️
Best Time to Visit
October to February is ideal for most hidden places — comfortable temperatures, clear skies, and good waterfall flow. Monsoon (June–September) is dramatic but limits access to treks and caves.
🧭
Get a Local Guide
For Pakshipathalam, tribal village visits, and the more remote valleys, a local guide isn't optional — it's what transforms a walk into an experience. Ask your guesthouse or contact Wayanad Tourism directly.
📜
Forest Permits
Pakshipathalam and some Brahmagiri trails require advance permits from the Forest Department. Apply at the Mananthavady range office or check KFRI Thrissur for online options.
🍽️
Eat Like a Local
Skip the resort buffets for at least one meal. The best Wayanad food is found in small hotels in Kalpetta and Sultan Bathery markets — kanji (rice gruel), puttu, pazhampori, and locally grown black pepper chicken.
🤫
Respect Tribal Villages
Tribal settlements are not tourist attractions. Visit only with community permission and a guide who has an existing relationship with the village. Unsolicited photography is disrespectful and often unwelcome.
🚗
Getting Around
A self-drive car or hired cab is essential for most of these gems. KSRTC buses cover the main towns but hidden places like Tholpetty, Karlad Lake, and Pakshipathalam are not on any bus route.
